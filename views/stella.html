My definition of techno-house is a shrimp

By Harry Burke


The cover artwork of Susumu Yokota’s bewitching acid techno album Zen (1994, released under the pseudonym Ebi) features a flat blue cartoon of a shrimp. Different parts of the shrimp are annotated as if they are elements of an electronic drum pattern—hi hat, snare, clap, bass drum, et cetera. Above is the statement:

“my definition of techno-house is a shrimp.”

This dreamlike claim came to mind as Stella Zhong introduced me to her installation. Like Yokota, Zhong makes unlikely connections: in her installation, a pavilion is defined as a potato chip, a levitating disk is described as a pancake, and the gap between two walls is likened to a stubby grain of rice. For Zhong, space is a starchy and playful material.

This logic, along with some of the installation’s points of tension, suggests an artistic engagement with derealization. For one thing, Zhong has labored to negate the gallery by building a large, immersive container. A beach of died sand surrounds this structure. These elements of the installation detach it from its immediate environment, and create a psycho-spatial distancing effect. They subtly displace their viewer.

Further, Zhong’s unwieldy object resists compartmentalization. The installation is by turns a theatrical, architectural, and sculptural proposition. Like a pearl in an oyster, at its center is an intimate, hidden cinema. Lying on the floor, their head upon a firm, angled pillow, visitors can watch a looping, eight-minute-long video. Audio hums from the speakers of a portable projector encased in a sand and epoxy shell. I was struck by the extent to which I encountered this soundscape as a spatial experience. This sensation further recalled acid techno: external space merged with interior feeling.

Derealization can be hard to describe. Playing hard to get, Zhong’s installation seeks to find an aesthetic language for a suspended sensorium. In doing so, it gestures toward a definition of sculpture as haptic—as felt. It fantasizes dissociation as an affect. I’m listening to Zen as I type these words. I’m eating rice with my eyes on my computer screen. The food in my body holds me in my body. Yokota was onto something.
